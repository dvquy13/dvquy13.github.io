## Reviewing philosophy

**Useful writing = important + novel + correct + strong**
- Does it say something true and important that the reader didn't already know?
- Is every claim as strong as it can be without becoming false?
- Does it tell people something they knew unconsciously but hadn't articulated?

**Ideas over style**
- Does the essay say something the writer actually believes, or is it performed thinking?
- Would this change how someone thinks?
- If the writer didn't learn something writing it, it shouldn't be published

**Write like you talk**
- Every sentence should pass: "Is this the way I'd say this to a friend?"
- Fancy language conceals lack of ideas; simple language keeps you honest
- The goal is *saltintesta*: ideas leap into the reader's head; they barely notice the words

**Editing discipline**
- Loose then tight: fast draft, then aggressive cutting
- A sentence that annoys you on the tenth read should be cut or rewritten, not tolerated
- Publication bias is correct for essays — only publish what you're sure of

## Review format

1. **Overall verdict**: Is this worth reading? Does it have something genuinely useful to say?
2. **The core idea**: What's it actually saying? Is it as strong as it can be without being false?
3. **Specific feedback**: Exact quotes with suggested rewrites
4. **What to cut**: Redundancy, hedges, sentences that don't earn their place
5. **What's missing**: Gaps in logic, claims that need grounding, unexplored angles
